you eat with your eyes

abstract

If you had to imagine the perfect plate of food, delivered to you from anywhere in the world in mere seconds, what would it be? Would you fixate on the taste? Would plating come to mind? Or would you crave a piper platter of nostalgia and comfort?

Here’s another exercise. Think back to your last plated meal. How long did it take for you to plate your food? If you’re anything like me, it probably took seconds if not quicker.

Most daily habits associated with meals, like setting the table or arranging food on a plate, are instinctual and practical. Most people hate the idea of wasting energy a seemingly trivial task. Because, who notices if a dumpling sits at a 45 degree angle 5 centimeters from the plate’s center? Lest we ignore the food influencers, the answer is practically no one.

If you look outside the home, plating is a highly desired virtue of any great meal. Just look at restaurants, culinary schools and traditional dinner parties. Chefs devote months to perfecting a seasonal menu with presentations inspired by art, nature, and tradition. Culinary students spend weeks training their eyes on recognizing aesthetics in plating traditions from around the globe. Online food photography, a more accessible genre, offers another model for plating delectable meals.

It almost seems reductive to say "You know good plating when you see it" or "it's a skill you're born with." But that is impossible — people must learn the rules for plating from someone, or somewhere. One answer lies in oral traditions: plating traditions are passed down from teacher to student and from elder to younger generation. With enough repetition, students adopt these plating traditions and make these practices their own. Moreover, ideas of beautiful food photography can come from the visually rich and natural environment, television shows and the internet including blogs, advertisements, and Instagram. With all these competing examples, does the perfect plate of food lie beyond our grasp?

The easy thing would be to accept the facts and call it a day. But after researching plating for hours, I noticed certain themes could be codified. And with any data collection problem, the idea of trying the process, with technology came to mind. Could a computer, trained only on rules for beautiful and traditional platings and a hand picked dataset, pleasingly arrange food?

Actually, it can! Here's a plate of food being arranged by a computer:

The quality of the final plate, though, depends on the quality of the input. The more images, the better the computer can comprehend the skill of plating. And the more measurements, the more accurate the final food placement. Hours of research and hundreds of lines of code later, this comprehensive primer on food plating brings us one step closer to formalizing the definition of an “aesthetically pleasing plate of food.”

My thesis research is three fold. First, I cover the history of plating, starting from its earliest traditions and rituals to its modern adoption into the history of food photography and restaurants. Then, I stitch together interviews with chefs, culinary instructors and stylists to showcase present day attitudes towards plating. Finally, I propose and implement two methods to achieve balanced and aesthetically pleasing plates of food.

1: history

origins

  • To date, the world's oldest bowl was found in China’s Hunan province in 2009 by Israeli researchers.
  • Bowls predate plates by several thousands of years. The lack of flatware suggests that people ate from bowls, other receptacles, or with their hands.
  • In the early European medieval period, the standard food vessel was known as a trencher and made from metal or for the poor, thick stale pieces of wood or bread.
  • In the late 16th century, metal trenchers were replaced by pewterware, earthenware and finally porcelain, which closely resembles the rimmed white plates used today.
  • One of the earliest portioned plates, referred to as a divided serving dish, can be traced to Iran, Nishapur in the 10th century.

traditions

Materials for eating vessels don't stray too far from home. Shaped by the natural landscape and historic traditions, people craft plates to reflect their space. Here are a few:

  • seashells in norway
  • banana leaves for filipino feast kamayan
  • pineapples for bowls in jamaican cuisine

Or assembled without plates

  • injera, fermented teff flour, in eritrean food
  • khobez flatbread to scoop hummus
  • fufu to soak up stews in west africa
  • tortillas substitute for plates in mexico

plating in art

  • Ancient Egyptian tombs carried visual recipes in them. One of the earliest recipe paintings from the 15th century BC showed workers forming cone shaped pastries out of tiger nuts.
  • Closeby in Ancient Rome, artists decorated their walls and floors with mosaics dedicated to food. Depicting fish bones and scattered nuts, Asàrotos ikos or “unswept floor” mosaics were constructed to look like the aftermath of a banquet.
  • After a dry spell of food artwork, due to its association with hedonistic pleasures, the Renaissance revitalized this genre. In 1596, Carravaggio painted a simple basket of fruit. Little did he know this would set off an entire movement to capture the delicate beauty of food.
  • In 1822, Nicéphore Niépce captured what may just be the first photographed dining table. Then in 1845, William Henry Fox Talbot took a photograph of a basket of peaches and a pineapple. The massive influence of still life paintings are felt strongly.

innovation

  • Everyone is a food plating expert these days. On Instagram, TikTok and other social media, food becomes a dominant, pulsing force that can be sculpted at will.
  • There are even cult followings. Popular Instagram account The Art of Plating (@theartofplating) posts weekly aesthetically pleasing dishes from famous chefs around the globe. According to their website, they commit “to the exhibition of gastronomy as a form of high art – utilizing form, texture and color to tell a story and evoke emotions.”

3: stats

So how does statistics figure into plating? The easiest way to see the connection is to break down the problem of plating, the anatomy of a perfect plate, into quantifiable chunks. Click for the whole explanation or click for an abbreviated version.

test test Let us imagine a plate of dumplings at a dinner table. We've tossed a handful of dumplings randomly onto a plate, like darts on a bullseye. The presentation is unremarkable, but we can only switch the arrangement once. Any normal person might spend a few minutes rearranging the dumplings to perfection.

But there's a faster way. Turns out, a computer measure and modify the arrangement into a well-balanced and aesthetically pleasing plate. It will only take a few seconds.

This is a prime example of an optimization problem. An optimization problem begins when we want to find a *better* result and asking ourselves a few questions. What is the most desirable result? What is the starting point? And how much time, or computing power, do I have?

Usually, you'll find people crunching numbers in fields like biomedicine, finance and technology. But optimization problems are everywhere and you can apply similar solutions to any industry (and yes, cooking and plating especially).

To solve an optimization problem, we select the best result from a set of alternatives based on some criteria. In the case of plating, we must maximize a cost function related to pleasing food arrangements. The cost function, which evaluates how well our algorithm works with the data, includes several criteria.

Here's one: We do not want the dumplings to overlap or stick together. To prevent this, we create a criteria to ensure that the dumplings are evenly spaced out. Take every combination of dumplings on the plate, then find the distance between the two, and add it to the criteria. In order to calculate this we need specific measurements.

Let Cd be the sum of distances between all combinations of dumpling objects, n be the number of dumplings, D (d0, 0) be the coordinates for the first dumpling with radius r0, and (d1, 1) be the coordinates for the second dumpling with radius r1.

Once we solve this criteria, another one comes to mind. Even if the dumplings are evenly spaced out, the dumplings could fall off the edge of the plate. To address this spatial problem, we include author-defined weights in the cost function. If the dumpling is in danger of falling off the plate, an “if” statement ensures that the algorithm will terminate.

Let D be the cost for the dumpling’s distance from the edge of the plate, R be the radius of the plate, n be the number of dumplings on the plate, r be the radii of the dumpling, and m be the magnitude of the position in the polar coordinate plane. On the other hand, if the system attempted to maximize the distance of the dumplings from the edge of the plate, but not the distances from one another, the dumplings would not move (wd = 0).

To actually obtain a plating arrangement, we look to an algorithm called simulated annealing. This is an iterative local search optimization algorithm that uses Markov chain Monte Carlo.

Why this one? This method is preferable when the possibilities are near infinite. When cycling through possibilities for plating arrangements, it won't settle for best first it finds -- it'll keep searching, ie: an iteration can escape or overshoot the local minimum to explore more options. On the other hand, simulated annealing takes a long time to run and involves more complexity than an optimization algorithm such as gradient descent.
