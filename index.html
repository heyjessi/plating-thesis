abstract

If you had to imagine the perfect plate of food, delivered to you from anywhere in the world in mere seconds, what would it be? Would you fixate on the taste or the plating? Or would your mind crave a piper platter of nostalgia and comfort?

Here’s another exercise. Think back to your last plated meal. How long did it take for you to plate your food? If you’re anything like me, it probably took seconds to plate if not quicker.

Most daily habits associated with meals, like setting the table or arranging food on a plate, are instinctual and practical. Most people abhor the idea of wasting intellectual energy a seemingly trivial task. Because, who notices if a dumpling sits at a 45 degree angle 5 centimeters from the plate’s center? Lest we ignore the food influencers, the answer is practically no one.

But if you look outside the home, plating is a highly desirable virtue of any great meal. Just look at restaurants, culinary schools and traditional dinner parties. Chefs devote months to perfecting a seasonal menu with presentations inspired by art, nature, and tradition. Culinary students spend weeks training their eyes on recognizing aesthetics in plating traditions from around the globe. Online food photography, a more accessible genre nowadays, offers another model to plate delectable meals.

With regards to plating, it seems too simplistic to say "You know it when you see it," especially when based around a person’s highly subjective opinion. So truthfully, no one has come up with one formal definition for what is aesthetically acceptable to everyone.

But after researching this topic for hours, I noticed certain themes that could be codified. In a world saturated with conflicting advice about food presentation, could a computer, trained only on rules for beautiful and traditional platings and this hand picked dataset, pleasingly arrange food? Hours of research and hundreds of lines of code later, this comprehensive primer on food plating brings us one step closer to formalizing the definition of an “aesthetically pleasing plate of food.”

Food stylist Victoria Granof once said of food plating, “It's just something that you're born with.” But that is impossible — people must learn the rules for plating from someone, or somewhere. One answer lies in oral traditions: plating traditions are passed down from teacher to student and from elder to younger generation. With enough repetition, students adopt these plating traditions and make these practices their own.

Ideas of beautiful food photography can also come from the visually rich and natural environment, television shows and the internet including blogs, advertisements, and Instagram. If humans can learn and internalize rules, surely a computer can follow a similar procedure, or at the very least try to.

My thesis research is three fold. First, I cover the history of plating, starting from its earliest traditions and rituals to its modern adoption into the history of food photography and restaurants. Then, I use interviews with chefs, culinary instructors and stylists to create a comprehensive about present day attitudes towards plating. Finally, I proposed and implemented two methods to achieve balanced and aesthetically pleasing plates of food.
